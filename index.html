9-Year Linux Kernel Flaw CVE-2026-46333 Enables Root on Debian, Ubuntu, and Fedora — PoC Released

Qualys discovered CVE-2026-46333 (CVSS 5.5), a privilege management flaw in the kernel's __ptrace_may_access() function that was introduced in November 2016 and went undetected for nine years. An unprivileged local user can exploit it via four different attack paths — chage, ssh-keysign, pkexec, and accounts-daemon — to read /etc/shadow, extract SSH host keys under /etc/ssh/*_key, and execute arbitrary commands as root on default installations of Debian, Ubuntu, and Fedora. A proof-of-concept exploit dropped alongside the public kernel commit last week, making exploitation trivial for anyone with local access.

Update your kernel immediately on all Linux servers and workstations. If patching isn't possible now, set kernel.yama.ptrace_scope to 2 as a temporary workaround — this blocks the ptrace-based attack path entirely.

The flaw affects on-premises Exchange 2016, 2019, and Subscription Edition at any update level; Exchange Online is not vulnerable. An attacker sends a crafted email that, when opened in OWA under certain interaction conditions, executes arbitrary JavaScript in the victim's browser context. CISA's federal remediation deadline under BOD 22-01 is tomorrow, May 29 — putting agencies in the awkward position of needing to "remediate" a vulnerability for which the vendor has not shipped a fix.

Colorado Repeals & Replaces AI Act — SB 26-189 Strips Algorithmic Discrimination Duty, Delays Enforcement to 2027

Governor Polis signed SB 26-189 on May 14, fully repealing and replacing the original 2024 Colorado AI Act that had been the nation's most aggressive state-level AI law. The new version, effective January 1, 2027, removes the most burdensome requirements for developers: no more mandatory risk management programs aligned to NIST AI RMF or ISO 42001, no annual impact assessments within 90 days of deployment, and no duty to self-report algorithmic discrimination harms to the Attorney General. What remains are targeted transparency obligations — advance notice and post-decision disclosures for consequential automated decisions. The AG has stated he won't enforce even the new law until rulemaking concludes. Meanwhile, xAI has filed a motion for preliminary injunction (due June 11), adding another layer of uncertainty about what actually takes effect and when.

The Federal Trade Commission has opened an antitrust investigation into Arm Holdings' semiconductor licensing practices, according to Bloomberg. The FTC is examining whether Arm — whose architecture powers virtually every smartphone, tablet, and an increasing share of laptops and servers — may be illegally monopolizing parts of the semiconductor market by threatening to refuse or downgrade CPU blueprint licenses to customers while simultaneously developing competing chips of its own. This matters directly to Apple developers: Apple Silicon (every chip in iPhone, iPad, Mac, and Apple Watch) is built on Arm's architecture. Any disruption to Arm's licensing model could ripple through Apple's silicon roadmap, pricing, and the broader ARM ecosystem that NVIDIA, Qualcomm, and Samsung also depend on. South Korea's antitrust regulator is running a parallel investigation.
