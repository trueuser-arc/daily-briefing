macOS.Gaslight: North Korea-Linked Rust Backdoor Uses Prompt Injection to Trick AI Malware Analysts

SentinelOne discovered a previously undocumented Rust-based macOS implant called Gaslight, attributed with high confidence to North Korean threat actors. The malware's standout feature is an embedded cascade of 38 fabricated "system" messages designed to make LLM-assisted triage agents doubt their own session — attacking the analyst's AI perception rather than the sandbox. Gaslight uses a Telegram bot API for C2, enters a polling loop for interactive shell access, and deploys a 6.6 KB Python info-stealer that harvests Terminal histories, Keychain databases, browser data from Chrome, Brave, Firefox, and Safari, and running processes — compressing everything into a ZIP uploaded via Telegram. Persistence is achieved through a LaunchAgent masquerading as "com.apple.system.services.activity." This is a warning shot: as security teams increasingly rely on AI-assisted analysis, adversaries are now designing malware specifically to defeat those tools.

If your security team uses LLM-based triage, this is the threat model you need to prepare for. Gaslight doesn't try to evade AI analysis — it actively gaslights it with fake system errors. Audit your AI analysis pipelines for prompt injection resilience, and ensure human analysts review AI-flagged "benign" samples.

IBM Debuts World's First Sub-1nm Chip — 0.7nm Nanostack Packs Nearly 100 Billion Transistors

IBM unveiled the world's first sub-1-nanometer chip technology at VLSI 2026, demonstrating a 0.7nm (7 angstrom) architecture called nanostack that packs nearly 100 billion transistors onto a chip the size of a fingernail — roughly double the density of IBM's 2021 2nm demonstration. Rather than shrinking transistors further in two dimensions, nanostack goes vertical: stacking and staggering transistors through 3D sequential integration, with different material combinations per layer allowing independent tuning of each transistor's performance and power characteristics. IBM demonstrated 40% SRAM scaling, enabling significantly more efficient chip designs for AI workloads. This is a research demonstration, not a product — commercialization timeline remains undisclosed while IBM focuses on helping partners scale today's 2nm nanosheet technology — but it signals that Moore's Law has at least another decade of runway.

The nanostack approach matters more than the node number. By going vertical instead of smaller, IBM sidesteps the physical limits that were supposed to end transistor scaling. MIT Technology Review estimates this could extend Moore's Law another decade — meaning the AI compute trajectory has more room than skeptics predicted.
