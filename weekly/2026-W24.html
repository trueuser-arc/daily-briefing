Anthropic Calls for Coordinated Global AI Development Pause — Warns Models Approaching Recursive Self-Improvement

In a rare public statement ahead of its IPO, Anthropic warned that AI task-completion capability is doubling roughly every four months and that the industry is approaching "recursive self-improvement" — the point where AI can improve itself faster than humans can oversee the process. The company called on all major labs to consider a coordinated, verifiable pause in frontier model development. This is the first time a leading AI lab has issued a formal call for an industry-wide slowdown, and it arrives at an awkward moment: Anthropic is simultaneously preparing to go public at a nearly $1 trillion valuation.

Watch how other labs respond. If Google or OpenAI reject the proposal, Anthropic's call becomes a differentiator for safety-conscious enterprise buyers rather than an actual slowdown. Either way, this signals the frontier capability curve is steeper than public benchmarks suggest.

June Patch Tuesday Tomorrow: Exchange Zero-Day Fix Expected, Secure Boot Certificates Expire June 24

Tomorrow's Patch Tuesday is unusually high-stakes. Microsoft is expected to finally ship a permanent fix for the actively exploited Exchange OWA zero-day CVE-2026-42897, but the real urgency is the Secure Boot certificate rollover: the 2011-era Microsoft UEFI CA expires June 24, giving enterprise teams just 15 days after patch deployment to validate and push new 2023 certificates across their entire fleet. Devices that miss the deadline lose the ability to receive boot-level security updates, trust new third-party UEFI drivers, or perform future full-OS upgrades. Windows Server requires manual certificate updates — it does not receive them automatically.

Canvas LMS Breach: 275M Records Exposed in Largest Education Data Breach on Record

ShinyHunters exploited Instructure's Free-For-Teacher account program — which let educators create Canvas tenants without institutional verification — to exfiltrate 3.65 TB of data across 8,809 educational institutions worldwide. The group struck twice (April 29 and May 7), and Instructure paid the ransom on May 11 in exchange for "shred logs" confirming data destruction. Names, emails, student IDs, and inter-user messages were compromised. Instructure has since shut down Free-For-Teacher accounts entirely, but the incident now faces federal scrutiny.

Two OpenAI employee devices were compromised via the "Mini Shai-Hulud" supply chain attack that hit TanStack, Mistral AI, UiPath, and 170+ npm/PyPI packages in a six-minute window on May 11. Magecart Campaign Turns Stripe's Own API Into a Malware Command-and-Control Server

Sansec discovered a Magecart variant that stores its skimmer payload in Stripe customer metadata and exfiltrates stolen credit cards by writing them back to the attacker's Stripe account as fake customers. The loader runs via planted Google Tag Manager containers, and because both GTM and api.stripe.com are domains that e-commerce stores explicitly whitelist, the entire attack chain bypasses Content Security Policy rules and network filters. This is an elegant abuse of trusted infrastructure — if you run an e-commerce checkout, audit your GTM containers and look for unexpected Stripe secret keys in client-side scripts immediately.

Everest Forms Pro CVE-2026-3300 Under Active Exploitation — CVSS 9.8 RCE via eval() Injection

A critical RCE flaw in the Everest Forms Pro WordPress plugin allows unauthenticated attackers to execute arbitrary PHP via the Complex Calculation feature, which passes user input through sanitize_text_field() — a function that does not escape single quotes — before eval(). Over 29,300 exploitation attempts have been blocked since April. Attackers are creating backdoor admin accounts. A patch has been available since March in version 1.9.13.

Colorado Governor Signs SB 189 — AI Act Compliance Deadline Pushed From June 30 to January 1, 2027

Governor Polis signed the replacement AI Act on May 14, officially pushing the compliance deadline from this month to January 1, 2027. The revised law swaps the original duty-of-care framework for a narrower disclosure-only approach: notify users when they interact with AI, disclose adverse automated decisions within 30 days, correct inaccurate personal data on request, and provide human review for consequential decisions. Developers shipping AI features in apps distributed in Colorado now have six additional months to comply.
